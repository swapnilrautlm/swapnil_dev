All six full-length tests run on Bluebook (bluebook.collegeboard.org) under real, timed conditions. Summer tests sit on Fridays — they replace that day's normal study. The final test is a Saturday morning to mirror exam day exactly.

What to prioritize — Math

PriorityDomainWeightSkills to drillStart at
1Algebra~35%Linear equations (1 & 2 variables), systems of linear equations, linear functions, linear inequalitiesEasy → Med
2Advanced Math~35%Equivalent expressions, nonlinear equations & systems, nonlinear functionsMed → Hard
3Problem-Solving & Data Analysis~15%Ratios/rates/units, percentages, one- & two-variable data, probability, inference from samples, statistical claimsMedium
4Geometry & Trigonometry~15%Area & volume, lines/angles/triangles, right triangles & trig, circlesEasy → Med

Algebra + Advanced Math are ~70% of the section — that's where the points are. Lock Algebra to near-perfect first (quick wins), then pour time into Advanced Math, where most score gains hide.

What to prioritize — Reading & Writing

PriorityDomainWeightSkills to drillStart at
1Standard English Conventions~26%Boundaries (punctuation, sentence breaks); Form, Structure & Sense (agreement, verb tense, pronouns, modifiers)Easy → Med
2Craft & Structure~28%Words in Context, Text Structure & Purpose, Cross-Text ConnectionsMedium
3Information & Ideas~26%Central Ideas & Details, Command of Evidence (text + data), InferencesMed → Hard
4Expression of Ideas~20%Transitions, Rhetorical SynthesisMedium

Conventions is rule-based, so it gives the fastest, most reliable gains — start there. Words in Context rewards steady vocabulary work. Inference and Command of Evidence improve more slowly, so build them through the back half.

Difficulty strategy. The digital SAT is adaptive: how she does on Module 1 decides whether Module 2 is the easier or the harder version — and the harder version has a higher score ceiling. So lock accuracy on Easy and Medium first (they're the majority and they route her into the high-ceiling module), then train Hard questions to lift the top end.

Daily rhythm

Summer weekdays (Mon–Fri)

  • 2 hours Math + 2 hours English, in focused blocks.
  • Run each hour as ~50 min drilling a target skill, then ~10 min logging and re-working every miss.
  • Split the day so the two subjects don't blur — e.g. Math in the morning, English after lunch.

Weekends & school weeks

  • Weekends: light, 2 hours max — error-log review, vocabulary, or rest.
  • From Sep 2 (school 7–3): ~1–1.5 hours in the evening, mostly review and short timed sets.
  • Fri Sep 11: very light, then rest before exam day.

Daily fuel — the Educator Question Bank

Use College Board's Educator Question Bank to build a targeted set for each day's skill — it holds 1,756 Math and 1,688 Reading & Writing official questions, plenty for the whole summer without repeats.

  1. Open Create a Question Set. Step 1: Assessment = SAT. Step 2: Section = Math or Reading and Writing.
  2. Step 3: tick the Domain for today's focus (e.g. Algebra, or Standard English Conventions), then Search.
  3. On the results page, use Add Filters → Skill to pick the exact skill, and Difficulty to set the level. Difficulty shows as filled bars: 1 = easy, 2 = medium, 3 = hard.
  4. Select ~10–15 questions, then Export a PDF with answers & explanations.
  5. Work the set, mark every miss, and copy the misses into the error log to re-attempt later in the week.

Master Desmos — until it's reflex

The built-in Desmos calculator is available in both math modules — there is no no-calculator section. On hard or messy questions, graphing is faster and safer than algebra. Practice on the exact calculator inside Bluebook, not just desmos.com, so the moves are muscle memory by exam day.

GoalWhat to do in DesmosGreat for
Solve an equationType y = f(x); click where it crosses the x-axisroots, zeros, "solutions", x-intercepts
Solve two equations / a systemType both on separate lines; click each intersection point for its coordinateslinear or nonlinear systems
Count solutionsGraph both; count the intersection points"how many solutions does the system have?"
Vertex / max / minGraph the parabola; click the turning pointvertex form, maximum/minimum, axis of symmetry
InterceptsClick where the curve meets each axisy-intercept (x=0), x-intercepts
Evaluate a functionDefine f(x)=…, then type f(3) on a new line"what is f(3)?", checking values
Add a sliderType an equation with a letter, e.g. y = ax² + 3; tap "add slider" and drag"for what value of k…", matching a condition visually
Line / curve of best fitEnter data as a table (x₁, y₁), then type y₁ ~ m x₁ + b using the tilde ~. Exponential: y₁ ~ a b^x₁two-variable data, "which equation models the data"
StatisticsPut data in a list, then mean(L), median(L), stdev(L), total(L)one-variable data: center & spread

Habits that make it reflex

  • Snap decision each question: messy numbers, multi-step, "how many solutions", or best-fit → graph it. One-step → do it by hand (graphing can be slower for the trivial ones).
  • Type cleanly: watch parentheses, and use the arrow keys to step out of a fraction or exponent before typing the next part.
  • One equation per line — each Enter starts a new expression.
  • Lost the intersection? Scroll/pinch to zoom out, or set the window with the wrench icon.
  • Drill it: in each phase, spend one block re-solving a question set entirely in Desmos until the moves are automatic.
This is version 1, built before the Jul 1 baseline. Once her diagnostic scores come in, the phases re-weight toward her weakest domains — the priorities above are the sensible starting order for a strong-math student building reading speed, not the final word.
